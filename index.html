Your Identity Map

What makes you you? The groups you belong to, the roles you play, the values you hold: these identities shape what you believe and who you trust. They also shape your biases. Social media algorithms are designed to amplify certain identities over others, quietly narrowing what feels true. This exercise makes that structure visible — so you can recognize your biases, become more resilient to misinformation, and engage more thoughtfully in our information ecosystem.

For reflection and educational purposes only. The percentages and scores in your results are rough estimates meant to help you visualize how your identities interact — not precise measurements. Think of your identity map as a mirror, not a diagnostic tool.

What your map reveals
Evidence-Based Insights — Roccas & Brewer (2002)

This exercise is grounded in research on social identity complexity — the degree to which your group memberships overlap. When your identities cluster together, sharing the same communities, values, and information sources, your identity map is less complex. When they are more distinct, your map is more complex. Research suggests that people with more complex identity maps tend to show greater tolerance toward outgroups and reduced ingroup bias. This exercise applies that insight to how we consume information — the more our identities cluster together, the more we may unconsciously favor information that confirms them.

Social media adds another layer: algorithms actively amplify certain identities, quietly shaping which version of you gets reinforced online. Your susceptibility to misinformation isn't only about who you are — it's about which version of yourself your feeds constantly reflect back to you.

Try different identities or platforms to see how the map changes.
